Energetski Certifikat

The energetski certifikat (energy performance certificate) is a mandatory document in Croatia that rates a building's energy efficiency on a scale from A+ to G and must be provided to buyers and tenants before a property transaction or rental.

Croatia implemented the energy performance certificate requirement in line with EU Directive 2010/31/EU on the energy performance of buildings. Since 2013 the certificate has been compulsory for the sale, rental, and construction of buildings. It summarises the property's annual energy consumption for heating, cooling, hot water, and lighting.

The certificate is issued by an accredited energy auditor (ovlašteni stručnjak) and is valid for ten years, unless major energy-related renovations take place in the interim. The rating scale runs from A+ (most efficient) to G (least efficient). The document must be displayed in advertisements for the property and handed over to the buyer at completion.

A low energy rating does not prevent a sale but can affect buyer interest and negotiating power. Buyers should use the rating to estimate future utility costs and may negotiate a price reduction to offset the cost of planned energy improvements such as insulation, new windows, or a heat pump.

The energetski certifikat also includes recommendations for cost-effective improvements. These recommendations are not legally binding but are useful when planning renovations and may be required to obtain certain state subsidies for energy refurbishment through the Fond za zaštitu okoliša i energetsku učinkovitost (Environmental Protection and Energy Efficiency Fund).

Sellers who fail to provide a valid certificate to the buyer may face administrative fines. Buyers who discover after purchase that the certificate was forged or invalid may have legal recourse against the seller and the issuing auditor.

Validity10 years (unless major works undertaken)
Rating scaleA+ (best) to G (worst)
Who issues itAccredited energy auditor
When requiredSale, rental, or construction of buildings
Legal basisAct on Energy Efficiency in Buildings (ZPUGD)
⚠ Common MistakeSome sellers present an expired certificate or one from a previous owner. Always check the certificate's date, the property address, and the issuer's accreditation number. An invalid certificate means the legal requirement has not been met.
💡 Expert TipIf the certificate rating is E, F, or G, request an energy improvement report from an accredited auditor. Many renovation costs (insulation, windows, solar) qualify for EU-co-funded Croatian grants that can cover 30–80% of expenditure.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is an energy certificate required for a garage or storage unit?

Generally not for standalone non-heated storage units. Garages integrated into residential buildings may require one depending on their use. Your solicitor can confirm the specific requirement.

Can the buyer commission their own energy certificate?

Yes, but in practice the seller must provide one before the sale. Buyers may commission an independent audit for reassurance, particularly for older buildings.

Does a new-build come with an energy certificate?

Yes. Developers must obtain an energy certificate as part of the building permit process and hand it to the buyer on completion.
