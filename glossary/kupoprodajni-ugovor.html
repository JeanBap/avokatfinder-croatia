Kupoprodajni Ugovor (Purchase Agreement)

Croatia's property purchase agreement — establishes terms before notarization and official Land Registry registration.

Overview

A Kupoprodajni Ugovor (Purchase Agreement) is the agreement between a property buyer and seller establishing the essential transaction terms. It identifies the property using cadastral data, establishes the purchase price and payment schedule, specifies timelines for inspection and mortgage approval, describes the property's condition, and identifies contingencies. The preliminary purchase agreement creates binding contractual obligations but does not transfer legal ownership, which occurs only through notarization and Land Registry registration.

The purchase agreement should clearly identify the property (cadastral number, address, area), specify the purchase price and payment terms (including deposit amounts and payment schedule), establish deadlines for mortgage approval and property inspection, describe any encumbrances or property defects, and identify contingencies allowing withdrawal under specific circumstances. Both parties should have the agreement reviewed by a lawyer to ensure mutual protection.

Typical purchase agreement timelines provide 30-60 days for mortgage financing approval, property inspection and appraisal, and legal review, with final notarization and registration scheduled thereafter. Early termination clauses should specify remedies for breach (forfeiture of deposits, damages claims). Clear documentation of deadlines prevents ambiguity and facilitates dispute resolution.

The purchase agreement is binding on both parties and creates legal obligations to proceed to notarization and registration. Croatian law permits enforcement of these obligations through courts. If the seller refuses to proceed after acceptance of the buyer's deposit, the buyer may sue for specific performance of the agreement or damages.

Once the notarized Property Sale Contract (Ugovor o Prodaji) is signed and registered in the Land Registry, the preliminary purchase agreement is fulfilled. The purchase agreement remains important evidence of the original transaction terms and may be referenced if disputes arise about the parties' intent or unperformed obligations.

Key Facts

  • Initial binding agreement establishing transaction terms before notarization
  • Should include property identification, price, payment terms, and timeline
  • Includes contingencies for financing, inspection, and legal review
  • Creates contractual obligations but does not transfer ownership
  • Ownership transfers only upon notarization and Land Registry registration

Common Mistake

Treating the purchase agreement as final — legal transfer occurs only through the notarized contract and Land Registry registration.

Pro Tip

Include specific deadlines for contingencies (financing approval, inspection) to provide clear enforcement mechanisms and avoid disputes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a written purchase agreement necessary in Croatia?

While not always legally required, a written agreement is essential. It documents party intentions, protects both parties, and serves as evidence if disputes arise. Mortgage lenders typically require one.

What happens if the buyer cannot obtain financing?

If the purchase agreement includes a financing contingency, the buyer can withdraw without penalty if mortgage approval is denied. Without this clause, the buyer may breach the contract and be liable for damages or deposit forfeiture.

Can either party cancel a purchase agreement?

Cancellation is possible only with both parties' agreement or if specific conditions (contingencies) are not met. Unilateral cancellation without legal cause constitutes breach of contract.
